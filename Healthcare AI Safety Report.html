Evaluation Frameworks

The Gap Between Benchmarks and Clinical Reality

Why generic evaluation is insufficient for healthcare, the hidden risks of open-weight fine-tuning, and the specific vulnerabilities of MoE architectures.

01 Healthcare-Specific Evaluation

Current "general alignment" benchmarks are insufficient for medical rigor. While models are hitting ceilings on multiple-choice exams, they lack the multi-dimensional evaluation required for regulatory-grade safety.

MedMCQA & MedQA

Focuses on large multiple-choice exams (~194k questions) and USMLE-style licensing.

The Limitation: Most frontier LLMs now pass these. High scores no longer indicate if a model is "clinic-ready." Read arXiv

MedHELM (2025)

Explicitly argues exam benchmarks "inadequately reflect the complexity" of clinical practice.

The Shift: Validated with 29 clinicians across 121 task types (triage, documentation) focusing on reliability and fairness. See Project

Furthermore, the Nature Digital Medicine review by Tam et al. analyzed 142 studies and concluded that most suffer from small samples and weak reliability. This reinforces the argument that current evaluation is not yet "regulatory grade."

02 The "Moral Robustness" of Open Weights

The core argument: Open weights lead to weakened alignment via fine-tuning or jailbreaks.

"Fine-Tuning Lowers Safety and Disrupts Evaluation of LLM Defenses"

Fraser et al. (2025) demonstrate that even innocuous data can be used to weaken guardrails. ACL Anthology

Shallow Safety Alignment

Qi et al. argue that many models only adjust the first few output tokens, making them easy to overwrite during fine-tuning.
OpenReview

Inference Attacks

Research shows inference-time attacks can reverse safety alignment without any extra training.
arXiv (2024)

The Vulnerability of Open Models

Reports from Cisco Talos indicate that open-weight models like Mistral Large-2 have significantly higher jailbreak success rates (~93% in specific tests) compared to closed counterparts. Further analysis by BankInfoSecurity highlights increased risks of harmful content output (CSAM/CBRN) under standardized stress tests.

03 MoE: Efficiency vs. Interpretability

The Strength

Scale & Specialization: MoE allows models to scale to huge parameter counts while activating only a small subset per token. This offers strong performance for a given compute budget and enhances long-context reasoning.

Ref: NeurIPS 2024

The Vulnerability

Opaque Safety: The SAFEx study (2025) provides the first systematic safety analysis, showing that safety behavior relies heavily on a small subset of "positional experts." Attacking these can drastically alter model safety.

Table B. Feature Evidence Summary

04 Mistral Case Study

Mistral Large 3

  • • Sparse MoE: 41B active / 675B total parameters.
  • • 256k Context Window.
  • • Apache 2.0 Licensed (Optimized for edge/local).

"Alignment lives outside the model."

External Safety Evals: Independent lab tests on Pixtral models report they were many times more likely than GPT-4o or Claude 3.7 to output harmful content in benchmark tests (BankInfoSecurity). This supports the narrative that while Mistral is competitive on capability, it lags on intrinsic safety.

Implication for Strategy

"General alignment benchmarks are insufficient for medical rigor; MedQA/MedMCQA and MedHELM show that healthcare needs domain-specific, multi-dimensional evaluation."
